# NZS Capital: Resilience & Optionality Framework Reference

## Section 1 — S-Curve Classification Matrix

The core diagnostic tool. Every company sits somewhere on an S-curve;
the analyst's job is to locate it and assess trajectory.

| Zone | Slope Signal | Valuation Logic | Portfolio Role | Fragile To |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Gambling** | Unclear; S-curve may not develop | Binary; precision impossible | Distributed, small (<1%) | Lindy Effect — older incumbents more durable than they appear |
| **Optional** | Early convex; S-curve forming | Binary; upside asymmetry matters more than precision | Distributed tail, 0.5–2% | Failure to develop a repeatable business model |
| **Resilient + Optional** | Clearly convex; flat growth rate over long time | Duration of convexity is the key variable. FCF yield + LT growth rate = expected return. Precision matters least here | Concentrated head, 3–5% | Duration shortening / valuation stretch |
| **Value Trap** | Turning or clearly concave | Appears cheap vs. history; widening outcome range | Distributed small or avoid | Entrenched thinking; overconfidence in new growth initiatives; selective vision |
| **ROOTMO** | Multi-S-curve stacker | Market prices Optionality deep out-of-the-money while questioning Resilience — maximum analyst edge | Large concentrated, 3–5%+ with room to run | Failure to execute on the new S-curve; reclassification back to Value Trap |

---

**ROOTMO — Full Definition**

ROOTMO stands for **Resilience with Out-Of-The-Money Optionality**.

It is the most valuable classification in the framework and represents
the maximum point of analyst edge. A ROOTMO company has two things
simultaneously:

1. A Resilient core business — durable, long-duration growth, strong
   ROCE, negative feedback loops, genuine NZS. The core is not broken.

2. Optionality that the market is pricing at or near zero — an adjacent
   bet, a new S-curve, a product category or geography that exists in
   the business but has not yet achieved commercial velocity. The market
   either does not see it, does not believe it, or is discounting it
   entirely because it is also questioning the Resilience of the core.

The maximum analyst edge occurs when both mispricings are present
simultaneously: the market is wrong about the Resilience (undervaluing
the core) AND wrong about the Optionality (pricing the adjacency at
zero). Correcting either mispricing alone generates returns. Correcting
both generates nonlinear returns.

ROOTMO is distinct from a Value Trap in one critical way: the new
S-curve is being built from a position of strength — funded by a
resilient, cash-generative core — not from desperation or pressure on
a deteriorating core business. This distinction is the hardest
judgement call in the framework and is the primary purpose of
Dimension D9 in the impression assessment.

---

**Key insight on valuation:** The right question is never "is this cheap?"
It is: *How many narrow predictions does this valuation require us to make?*
A stock at 10x FCF requires few. A stock at 10x revenues requires many.
Prediction count is the real measure of fragility.

**The back-of-envelope return formula:**
> FCF Yield + Expected Long-Term FCF/share Growth Rate = Expected Return

This is not a DCF. It is a rapid filter to determine whether the stock
is worth deeper work and whether it is more dependent on growth or
current profitability.

**On slow growth:** The market serially undervalues slow, long-duration
growth. 15% growth over 10 years delivers 300%+ return. Over 15 years,
nearly double that. Time is an exponent. This is why Resilient compounders
with flat growth curves are the core of the portfolio, not the exciting names.

## Section 2 — Quality Assessment (Score 1–5 on Each Dimension)

### 2A. CEO Role: Capital Allocator vs. Operator

The single most important management question. Most CEOs focus on
efficiently running daily operations — decentralisation makes them
uncomfortable, so they default to tight central control.

The rarer and more valuable CEO understands their primary job is capital
allocation; operations are delegated to business unit leaders.

**Allocator signals:**
- Small HQ, large empowered BUs
- Compensation tied to long-term value creation, not quarterly EPS
- Track record of buying back stock intelligently, making accretive acquisitions,
  or returning capital when organic opportunities are scarce
- Language in annual letters focuses on per-share intrinsic value, not revenue growth

**Operator signals (caution):**
- Large centralised team, frequent founder/CEO involvement in daily decisions
- Heavy emphasis on quarterly and annual targets in communications
- Capital allocation appears reactive rather than deliberate

**India-specific nuance:** Promoter-run companies often centralise control
but bring genuine long-term orientation. Do not penalise centralisation alone.
Distinguish *centralised + long-term* (acceptable) from *centralised +
short-term / self-interested* (red flag). Related-party transactions are
the sharpest diagnostic here.

### 2B. Decentralised Decision Making

Decentralisation distributes mistakes (small, survivable). Centralisation
concentrates them (large, potentially fatal). The best way to generate
large gains in a complex system is to make as many small mistakes as
possible at the lowest cost per mistake.

**Evidence of genuine decentralisation:**
- BU P&L accountability married to authority (both must exist)
- Product and customer-facing decisions made close to the customer
- Small, cross-disciplinary teams; agile structure
- HQ focuses on capital allocation and strategic parameters, not day-to-day

### 2C. Long-Term Thinking

**Evidence:**
- Management articulates what will NOT change over the next decade
  (customer needs that are durable) rather than predicting what will change
- Capex horizon extends beyond 3 years
- Compensation structures avoid quarterly/annual overweighting
- Willingness to sacrifice short-term margins for long-term ecosystem health
- Under-promise, over-deliver pattern across cycles

**The Bezos test:** Can management clearly articulate what will remain
constant about their customers' core needs 10 years from now?

### 2D. Culture of Innovation and Adaptability

- All innovation is integral to the business, not segregated into a lab
- Company disrupts its own products before competitors do
- Focus on low-end and underserved customers (often the most durable growth)
- Incentives and structure promote adaptability, not entrenchment

## Section 3 — Growth / NZS Assessment

### Five Mandatory NZS Diagnostic Questions

These must all be answered, not selectively applied:

**1. Stakeholder Value Creation**
Does the business create significantly more value for customers, employees,
suppliers, and society than it captures for itself? Quantify where possible:
customer surplus, employee retention rates, NPS, supplier tenure.

**2. Win-Win Business Models**
Are relationships with ecosystem partners collaborative or extractive?
Do suppliers voluntarily offer best pricing/terms, or are they squeezed?
Does the company price well below the point of Pareto efficiency
(charging up to the point where the customer would seek alternatives)?

**3. Long-Term Orientation**
Does management explicitly articulate 5–10 year goals prioritising durable
value creation over quarterly earnings? Is this evident in capital allocation
decisions, not just investor day language?

**4. Resilience Indicators**
Does the company exhibit negative feedback loops (self-correcting, growth-
moderating mechanisms) rather than positive feedback loops (boom-bust, unchecked
hyper-growth)? Is revenue diversified across 100+ customers or 10+ end markets?
High customer concentration is a positive feedback loop risk.

**5. Optionality Assessment**
Does the company have small exploratory bets (<5% of its own capital) in
adjacencies that could unlock asymmetric upside? Are these bets genuinely
exploratory or are they dressed-up core capex?

### Feedback Loop Diagnosis

| Positive Feedback Loop | Negative Feedback Loop |
|---|---|
| Hyper-growth, unchecked | Slow, durable growth |
| High customer concentration | Diversified customer base |
| Boom-bust cycle risk | Self-correcting when stressed |
| Pine beetle / Burmese python pattern | Ant colony pattern |
| Eventually exhausts resources | Extends S-curve duration |

**The counterintuitive finding:** Quick growth is a warning sign.
Long, slow, steady growth is the signal of a genuinely resilient business.

### TAM and S-Curve Stacking

- TAM must be large enough that the company can compound without hitting
  a ceiling within the investment horizon
- Evidence of S-curve stacking: launching adjacent products or entering
  new verticals from a position of strength, not desperation
- Stacking from Resilience into Optionality is the ROOTMO pattern

## Section 4 — Context Assessment

### Valuation Fragility

Ask: How many predictions, and what kind, does this valuation require?

- **Duration of growth predictions** — most worth making; good analyst
  correlation; negative feedback loops and NZS make these relatively reliable
- **Future margin or profitability predictions** — more fragile; harder to predict
- **New market / new vertical success predictions** — most fragile; require
  the most narrow precision; should never anchor a large position

Companies past the mid-point of their S-curve will almost always be
overvalued even when they appear cheap relative to history. The valuation
appears precise; the outcome range is widening.

### Headwinds and Tailwinds

Focus on: what will change and what will not change in the ecosystem,
and how this affects the company's position. Not GDP forecasts or Fed
policy — those are noise. The signal is in the structural direction of
the ecosystem.

### India-Specific Overlays

These must be applied for any NSE/BSE-listed company:

- **Promoter quality:** Long-term track record of capital allocation.
  Has promoter ever raised dilutive equity at a bad price? Pledged shares?
  Diverted capital via RPTs?
- **RPT threshold:** Any RPT exceeding 5% of revenues is an automatic NZS
  deduction. It is the most direct evidence of value extraction from the
  ecosystem rather than value creation.
- **Liquidity band:** For smallcaps, illiquidity is itself a negative
  feedback loop — it naturally limits hyper-growth, extends S-curve duration,
  and depresses valuations. In the R&O framework this is a feature, not a bug.
  The liquidity discount is the price of admission for the duration premium.
- **Regulatory risk:** Sector-specific regulatory concentration (e.g.,
  government as primary customer, price-regulated products) must be
  explicitly flagged as a headwind variable.

## Section 5 — Calibration Benchmarks

Use these as reference points when assessing unfamiliar companies:

**Microsoft / Azure under Nadella — Canonical NZS**
Nadella explicitly articulated: *"Our business model is about creating more
surplus outside us."* Azure was deliberately underpriced vs. competitors to
build a partner ecosystem where partner revenues dwarf Microsoft's direct
earnings. NZS at scale. Pricing power sacrificed for ecosystem lock-in.

**Texas Instruments — Canonical Resilience**
Analog semiconductors with 10–20 year product lifecycles. 100,000+ customers
across automotive, industrial, personal electronics. Owns fabs (vs. fabless
competitors). 15 years of consistent capital allocation discipline. "Boring"
but essential products = negative feedback loop on disruption risk.

**Shopify — Canonical Optionality graduating to Resilience**
Initially sized at 1–2% (tail position). Democratising e-commerce for SMBs
was a broad, safe prediction; whether Shopify would execute was the narrow one.
As merchant retention exceeded 90% and revenue CAGRs sustained above 50%,
the position graduated to a larger head position. This is the ROOTMO
graduation pattern in practice.

## Section 6 — Common Mistake Flags

Flag any of the following when encountered:

- **Mistaking commitment for conviction:** Anchoring on valuation while the
  core business deteriorates. The stock gets cheaper; the analyst adds. Classic
  value trap behaviour.
- **Optionality oversized:** A name that belongs in the tail (0.5–2%) has
  drifted to 4–5% due to performance. Harvest the gain; restore the sizing.
  One 50mph crash is fatal. Fifty 1mph crashes are an annoyance.
- **Stuck in the middle:** Resilient characteristics but no S-curve stacking
  ability and no optionality. These will be market performers at best and
  cannot justify concentrated positions.
- **Round-tripping:** Buying a Value Trap, watching it recover, calling it
  Resilient without genuine re-underwriting.
- **Confirmation bias dressed as research:** All data points confirm the
  thesis. No genuine adversarial testing. Naysayers have been filtered out.
